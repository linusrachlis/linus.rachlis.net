The end falls out on you

In ancient Rome they plunged their hands in.
Washing off the blood.
Eating apples and spectating the stench.

Nowadays I put down my spoon till it’s over.

Yet out of my science I envision a God, and hope
he expects that we’ll all make the worst mistakes
right before we grow up.

Nowadays when we choose to be stupid, it makes us principled.

Only certain of us retained the ability
to burn cities to the ground
and eat an apple.

What do the rest of us get?
a persistent toothache from our hidden carnivorous ways;
a hand-held heart for all those other times, which have become every time;
a broadband connection to images of every unbidden kind at a speed without precedent;
multifaceted covariant bilingual extra-ordinary penpersonship;
the ability to smirk uncontrollably while striding through a public place,
    then later forget why.

We’ve cut all our ribbons.
The ragged edges flail in the wind, caressing briefly at the old incision.
No one watches.

Nowadays, I do my apple-eating at my desk, where the juice,
    though spattering uncontrollably,
    will not return home till all this
    comes crumbling down.

Still, can we be saved?
We have found moments of true union, even though afterwards the priest
    goes out back for a smoke break and the poet swears artlessly in the
    heavy traffic on the way home from the reading. She just wants a shower
    and that leftover stirfry, and refuses to be observant any more until she’s
    spectated some TV and slept a few hours.

Peeking through the skinbag, the animal,
meek and furtive.
The Romans would not understand.

You were too young to remember:
when the first circle gathered
apples were a sacrament.
The whiffs sent messages.
That was a circle I could understand.
You know?
Just people. In a circle.

The curves apparent;
the meat killed and cooked in the centre for all to see.
If a single person broke away
the fire would surely flicker out.

Now each
         deeper circle
                     finer fabric
                              vaster still
                                          till
                                          no single
                                          set
                                          of eyes
                                          can see
                                          the curve.
